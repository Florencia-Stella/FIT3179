Air Quality Matters: Indonesia's Path to Cleaner Environments

Air Quality Index

Breakdown by Province

Air Quality Index (AQI) serves as a universal yardstick for quantifying and conveying the extent of air pollution within a given geographical area. In the diverse landscape of Indonesia, AQI measurements exhibit notable disparities among its various provinces. Metropolises such as Jakarta and the Java region contend with elevated AQI values due to the amalgamation of industrial emissions and the congestion of vehicular traffic.

In this visualization, it is imperative to recognize that a lower AQI Category signifies a disconcerting state of air quality, indicating higher levels of pollution. Conversely, a healthier atmosphere is represented by a higher AQI Category, featuring lower pollution levels.

Air Pollution

Distribution of Population-Weighted PM 2.5

A common parameter used to assess air quality is the concentration of particulate matter. Among these, PM2.5, referring to particulate matter with a diameter of 2.5 micrometers or less, stands out as a fine particle with known health hazards. These fine particles can penetrate deep into the respiratory system, posing significant risks to human health.

Remarkably, despite its modest size, Jakarta, Indonesia's capital city, records the highest levels of PM2.5 concentration. This is followed closely by the province of North Sumatra, emphasizing the critical need for attention to air quality management in these regions.

Sector-Wise Greenhouse Gas Emissions

Greenhouse gases (GHGs) wield a significant influence on climate change, posing environmental challenges that Indonesia grapples with. Notably, the country's key sectors play a substantial role in these emissions.


Fuel exploitation, centered around fossil fuel extraction and usage, stands out as a major source of methane (CH4) emissions , a potent GHG. Curbing emissions from this sector is pivotal for ensuring environmental sustainability.


Indonesia's power industry significantly contributes to arbon dioxide (CO2) emissions, emphasizing the urgent need for a transition to cleaner, more sustainable energy sources.


In the agricultural sector, nitrous oxide (N2O) emissions underscore the importance of reducing emissions from practices like enteric fermentation and rice cultivation. These efforts are crucial for mitigating Indonesia's carbon footprint and steering the nation towards a greener, more sustainable future.

Impact

Daily Average Temperature

The interplay between air quality and temperature trends in Indonesia from 2018 to 2020 is a critical environmental consideration. Deteriorating air quality, driven by increased pollutant levels and greenhouse gases, has a direct impact on rising temperatures. During this period, Indonesia witnessed a noticeable uptick in temperatures, closely correlated with the decline in air quality.

Heightened pollutant levels, predominantly resulting from industrial activities and vehicular emissions, intensified the greenhouse effect, trapping heat within the Earth's atmosphere and exacerbating global warming. Recognizing this connection underscores the urgent need to address air quality and greenhouse gas emissions, advocating for sustainable practices and policies to ensure a more climate-resilient future for Indonesia.

Top 5 Cause of Deaths

Air pollution has emerged as an alarming and persistent threat, securing its place among the top three causes of death in Indonesia. Amidst a diverse landscape of health challenges, from chronic diseases to accidents, air pollution steadfastly maintains its position. This unsettling reality underscores the immediate and long-term health consequences of exposure to polluted air. As one of the leading factors contributing to mortality, it not only claims lives but also calls for proactive measures and comprehensive policies to mitigate its impact.

Trends in Air Pollution-Related Deaths

In examining the landscape of air pollution-related deaths, it becomes evident that household pollution stands as the most pressing concern, consistently ranking as the leading cause of fatalities. Despite the myriad factors contributing to air pollution-related mortality, household pollution's steadfast prominence cannot be overlooked. This alarming trend reflects the significant health risks posed by indoor air quality and household cooking practices.
